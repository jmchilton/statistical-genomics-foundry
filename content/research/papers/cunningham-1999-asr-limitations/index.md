## 3. Thesis (1 sentence)
Reconstructed ancestral character states are a weak basis for testing certain evolutionary hypotheses — especially hypotheses of irreversible evolution (Dollo's law) — because the characters used are typically under selection and thus prone to homoplasy, making ancestral-state inference for individual characters harder and less reliable than phylogeny inference. (Stated in the p. 665 introduction; the paper says it illustrates this with two echinoderm life-history case studies.)

## 4. Problem & context (from p. 665)
- The explosion of phylogenetic information has spread beyond systematics; workers across disciplines generate phylogenies and use them to reconstruct ancestral character states, "usually by parsimony."
- The appeal is simplicity: testing evolutionary hypotheses by mapping characters onto a phylogeny is attractive and widely adopted.
- Counterpoint the paper presses: ancestral-state reconstruction is an "ambitious exercise." Even though inferring phylogenies from thousands of characters is hard (Swofford et al. 1996), reconstructing the ancestral state of an *individual* character is correspondingly *more* difficult (cites Swofford & Maddison 1987, 1992; Maddison & Maddison 1992; Collins et al. 1994a; Frumhoff & Reeve 1994; Pagel 1994; Maddison 1995; Schluter 1995; Schultz et al. 1996; Omland 1997; Schluter et al. 1997; Cunningham et al. 1998).
- Key framing argument (p. 665): data used for phylogeny are generally assumed selectively neutral, but many/most characters reconstructed to test evolutionary hypotheses are thought to be under selection (Brooks & McLennan 1991; Harvey & Pagel 1991) — indeed their presumed selective importance is why they are of interest. Therefore the convergence and parallel-evolution (homoplasy) problems that plague phylogenetic inference "should be that much more serious when we test evolutionary hypotheses with ancestral state reconstructions."

## 5. Method / approach
- Stated approach (p. 665): a "Points of View" argument piece. The author states he discusses the limitations of using reconstructed ancestral character states to test evolutionary hypotheses, arguing specifically that hypotheses of irreversible evolution (e.g., Dollo's law; Dollo 1893) are particularly difficult to test this way, and that he illustrates the point "with two case studies of life history evolution in echinoderms."
- Case study 1 setup visible on p. 665 — section heading "DOLLO'S LAW AND THE BIASED LOSS OF LARVAL FEEDING IN ECHINODERMS":
  - Strathmann (1978, 1985, 1993) argued obligate larval feeding has been repeatedly lost across echinoderm evolution; larval feeding appears homologous among echinoderm classes and dates back at least to the Ordovician; Strathmann attributes the absence of larval feeding in many echinoderms (including the entire Class Crinoidea) to a unidirectional bias of repeated losses.
  - Simpson's (1953) reformulation of Dollo's law: once lost, complex characters should be difficult to regain (a complex character's expression can be interrupted by a few genes; unused developmental pathways become progressively harder to restore — Raff et al. 1990; Maddison 1994; Omland 1997).
  - Echinoderm larval feeding requires a ciliary system and a full digestive tract, so its loss "certainly qualifies as the loss of a complex character" (Strathmann 1978).

## 6. Key claims / findings
From the accessible first page only:
- Ancestral-state reconstruction is most commonly done by parsimony; its appeal is the simplicity of mapping characters onto a phylogeny. [p. 665]
- Reconstructing the ancestral state of a single character is *harder*, not easier, than reconstructing a phylogeny from many characters. [p. 665]
- Characters chosen to test evolutionary hypotheses are usually under selection (unlike the near-neutral characters assumed for phylogeny building), so homoplasy (convergence, parallelism) is a *more* severe problem for hypothesis tests based on ancestral states than for phylogeny inference itself. [p. 665]
- The paper's central thesis: irreversibility hypotheses (Dollo's law) are especially difficult to test with ancestral-state reconstruction. [p. 665]
