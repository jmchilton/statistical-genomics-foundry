# StatReviewer + SciScore

> **statreviewer.com · sciscore.com** — StatReviewer: "automated review of statistical and reporting integrity for scientific manuscripts"; SciScore: "the best methods review tool for scientific research."
> Two deployed, commercial, automated manuscript referees that run inside journal editorial pipelines. StatReviewer runs "thousands of algorithmic tests" over a submitted manuscript — numerical-consistency errors, quality of reporting, whether the reported test fits the data (e.g. "t-test for skewed data"), style, methodological reporting — and emits an editor/author checklist inside Editorial Manager. SciScore scans a methods section with NLP (published as conditional-random-field models plus regex named-entity recognition) against public rigor checklists — MDAR, ARRIVE, CONSORT, NIH rigor, RRIDs — and returns three reports plus a 1–10 score; journal-year averages form its Rigor and Transparency Index. Both are closed/commercial SaaS; SciScore lists Science, bioRxiv/medRxiv, AHA Journals, Charité, and CZI among clients. Verified 2026-06-27 against sciscore.com + the asedasciences "about" page, the SSP/Aries Editorial-Manager integration announcement, the BMC "peerless review" blog, and the Rigor-and-Transparency-Index papers (iScience / PMC). StatReviewer's own domain did not resolve at verification time; its claims are grounded in the integration and press sources.

## Its framing

Both present as automated *pre-review* referees that augment, not replace, human peer review — explicitly analogized to how plagiarism software (CrossCheck) sits ahead of review. StatReviewer's stated ambition is *not* to judge the quality of the science but to check the reporting: it flags obvious numerical errors, missing replication detail, and mismatches between the data and the reported statistical test, then hands editors a checklist and author-ready feedback. SciScore's stated mission is "enhancing scientific rigor and transparency": it reads the materials-and-methods section across ten dimensions (ethics, group selection, demographics, reporting standards, replication, cell-line authentication, protocol/RRID identifiers, code, data, euthanasia) and scores how completely the manuscript states the rigor details the community checklists ask for. Both run in roughly a minute, at manuscript scale, integrated into real submission systems (Editorial Manager, eJournalPress).

## Strengths relative to our approach

These are the closest thing to a *deployed, commercial automated statistical referee* — and on deployment they are far ahead of the Foundry:

- **Real market deployment at scale.** Integrated into the editorial systems most journals actually run (Editorial Manager, eJournalPress), processing live submissions today, with named publisher and institutional clients. The Foundry is a design workspace; this pair is in production. The very fact that journals pay for an automated statistical/reporting referee is evidence the referee role has genuine demand — a point that *supports* our thesis.
- **The referee role, in spirit, done for real.** StatReviewer goes beyond checklist presence: testing whether the reported method fits the data (a $t$-test on skewed data) is a genuine assumption-style check, not just "is a field filled in." That is the closest external gesture toward refereeing *method appropriateness* rather than reporting completeness.
- **Public, community-owned rubrics.** SciScore's checklists — MDAR, ARRIVE, CONSORT, NIH rigor — are open, human-authored, widely adopted standards. The judgment criteria (what a rigorous methods section must state) are inspectable even though the engine that applies them is not.
- **Published methodology and a derived metric.** SciScore's approach (CRF + regex NER) and its Rigor-and-Transparency-Index validation are described in peer-reviewed papers — more methodological openness than a pure black box, and a real attempt to make the output measurable and comparable across journals.

## Where it diverges from the Foundry

Running automated statistical referees at scale is a real similarity in spirit, and this pair proves the role has paying demand — credit fully given. Three concrete axes separate them from the Foundry. *Unit*: they referee a **written manuscript's reporting** — is the right detail stated, is the reported test consistent with the reported data — whereas the Foundry's referee judges **live method validity**: assumptions actually met, no double-dipping, the named method real and appropriate, error rate empirically controlled (null/permutation calibration, simulation under known truth, power). StatReviewer's "t-test on skewed data" check is the closest gesture, but it infers from reported text, not from the data and the analysis as run. *Timing*: they run **post-hoc on a finished paper** at submission; the Foundry's gate runs **in-loop**, on an arbitrary live analysis, before a result is ever certified. *Openness*: they are **closed, hosted, commercial** engines; the Foundry's referee is an **inspectable skill** cast from a readable source, that partly *constructs its own empirical check*. The crux: they check whether a paper *said* the right things about an analysis already done; the Foundry checks whether the analysis *is valid* while it is still being done.
