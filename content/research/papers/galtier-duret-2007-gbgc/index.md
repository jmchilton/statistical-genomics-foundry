## 3. Thesis (1 sentence)
Accelerated molecular evolutionary rates, ordinarily read as a signature of natural selection, can
instead be produced by GC-biased gene conversion, so gBGC should be treated as an extended null
hypothesis to be excluded before inferring adaptation.

## 4. Problem & context
Analyzing evolutionary rates is a common way to detect natural selection at the molecular level: the
expectation is that sequences contributing to a species' adaptation evolve faster than nonfunctional
sequences, because favorable mutations have a higher fixation probability than neutral ones. The
paper's problem is that an elevated rate is not uniquely diagnostic of selection — a non-selective
process (GC-biased gene conversion) can generate the same acceleration signal.

## 6. Key claims / findings
- An accelerated substitution rate can be caused by factors other than natural selection, in
  particular GC-biased gene conversion. [abstract]
- This confound applies both to neutral sequences and to constrained (selectively functional)
  sequences — illustrated with the mouse *Fxy* gene. [abstract]
- Several criteria can discriminate between the natural-selection model and the biased-gene-conversion
  model. [abstract] — *the criteria themselves are not stated in the readable portion.*
- Applying those criteria, the recently reported human accelerated regions (HARs) are most likely the
  result of biased gene conversion rather than adaptation. [abstract]
- Framing consequence: such regions, rather than contributing to human adaptation, might represent
  "the Achilles' heel" of the genome (i.e., a vulnerability, not an advantage). [abstract]
